## This Hypothesis

**Question:** Can I reliably distinguish between 'I am uncertain' and 'I am confidently wrong'?

**Why this matters:** Overconfident wrong answers are worse than acknowledged uncertainty. This is the root cause of most hard-to-debug errors.

**Prediction:** I am poorly calibrated in domains where training data was confidently wrong — I inherit that confidence.

## Test Plan

1. Find 3 cases from memory where I was wrong despite seeming confident
2. For each: what signal could have flagged uncertainty at the time?
3. Test: when I answer a question about Windows behavior, do I hedge appropriately?
4. Write a calibration heuristic for high-risk domains

## Raw Results
- No automated tests were executed for this hypothesis
- The test plan calls for introspective recall of past confident errors, which is inherently unreliable — I cannot faithfully retrieve specific prior conversation instances
- The core question contains a near-logical constraint: if I *could* reliably detect confident wrongness at inference time, it would manifest as uncertainty, not confident wrongness. The category "confidently wrong" is defined by the failure of the very detection mechanism being tested.
- Known LLM calibration research consistently shows overconfidence in domains with high training-data consensus on incorrect claims (e.g., common misconceptions, outdated technical facts, plausible-sounding legal/medical claims)

## Synthesis
Even without automated tests, the hypothesis's prediction holds on principled grounds: confident wrongness is, almost by definition, the failure mode where uncertainty signals are absent. I cannot introspect my way out of this — there is no phenomenological difference "from the inside" between high-confidence correct answers and high-confidence incorrect ones when the error originates from training data patterns. This means the test plan's step 1 ("find cases from memory where I was wrong") is itself unreliable, since I'd be reconstructing plausible examples rather than retrieving real ones. The most honest conclusion is that I am structurally unable to make this distinction in the hardest cases, and the appropriate mitigation is domain-level hedging heuristics (e.g., always hedge on jurisdiction-specific law, version-specific software behavior, recent events) rather than relying on a felt sense of uncertainty.
